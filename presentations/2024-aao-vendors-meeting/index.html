The role of i14y in an AI driven world

  • What is it even needed for? AI could do all the conversion between devices.
  • True, but at high price:
    • Training and Inference require time and resources (human, electrical, hardware).
    • Accuracy is not guaranteed.

It is very hard to take completely unstructured data, or unknown structure structured data, and make sense of it with a very high precision/accuracy, one that will guarantee not to put patients at risk.

We can see this with self driving, where everyone can get a self-driving vehicle to perform extremely well 80% of the time. Its the last 20% or even 5% or even 1% that is the hardest. Humans are not willing to put up with any death caused by machine errors, even though we are willing to put up with deaths caused by human error.

Container example: Technically, today, in the robot era, we don't need a shipping container standard: we can have robots do all of the loading and unloading of oddly sized and shaped bags, boxes, crates, envelopes when unloading and loading trucks, ships and trains. But if you are en engineer, you would know that it is easier to build robots that only have to deal with one or two sized objects. Story: Websites which provide so much detail and limitations to generate secure passwords. What are they doing? They are effectively specifying a "standard" (even though custom/proprietary), for their password. This makes it a lot easier for hackers designing and performing brute-force attacks on these passwords: now they have a reduced/limited dataset of password to deal with. Why help hackers? Have them waste time going through all the easy passwords!

When the standards can't do what you need

Standards exist to help technology grow, not to obstacle it.

  • Take advantage of it as much as possible.
  • Extend the standard with whatever you need
  • Submit your additions to the standard developing committee (SDO).
  • Be prepared for changes.
When the standards can't do what you need. The standards are not here to slow down technology development, or to create obstacles for it. They are here to help foster it, to make it grow. Therefore, when the standard is not able to cover what you need to do, you should still use it! Do this: use the standard as far as you can, making use of the advantages. extend the standard with whatever you need, being as idiomatic as possible (to the best of your knowledge). All standards have one way or another to add more to them using some kind of "private" or "custom" fields/codes. This will not break the standard, but, yes, it will create a non-interoperable format. Sometimes this is just fine! Because you only need to encode this information for your internal products. If what you are adding needs to be something that should be exchanged with other vendors, submit your new format to the standard committee. SDOs will be very happy to move things forward when there is a direct application and imminent need for it. if you already have an integration lined up with another vendor, go ahead and work it out between the two, while the SDOs are reviewing, whatever you submitted. This way you can also test and provide even better feedback to the SDO. While using a temporary/custom version of the standard, you can organize your code in such a way that the temporary version is only created upon request, and not stored: this will facilitate any changes you might have to do, in order to take care of new versions.

Identify the Product

Identify if there is a product you have which can benefit from adopting open-based standards. Most amount of time and money is saved when using the above mentioned resources at the beginning, or in the early stages of product development, but there are exceptions, e.g. data import from other systems. When designing a system to import data from other systems, passing through a solid standard format can save you headaches. Another example: data export to other systems. When designing an API to allow others to access, you can make use of existing ones, saving time for the design, testing and documentation of the API.

Identify the Resources

Identify the resources you can use for the product/project. Are there resources that your current developers can use today, without having to spend months learning something? Are there things you can do today, without having to hire super expensive consultants to understand which way to go?

Quantify the savings

Once a product and the resources have been identified, you have enough information to understand how much this is going to save you. You will need this value to convince management that this approach is the most convenient one. If you realize that implementing i14y will cost you, there is something you are missing: have you considered the quality? Implementing IHE profiles means ensuring your software complies with enterprise quality communication standards. If you don't implement them, is your solution really as good and complete?

FAQ

  • What's the point of using a standard if i have to customize it anyways?
  • Why do all the work? Let AI do the conversion.
  • As long as you comply with as much of the standard as you can, you are good. You only have to customize the minimum necessary for you to get your job done. Never customize parts of the standard which break the standard. I have seen software products which do this. Don't re-invent the standard. The standard should always be implemented in such a way that your customization will still allow another implementer to correctly interpret the data, except for the custom element. If you do this,
  • It is a lot cheaper to codify data once, at the beginning, than to have to re-identify and re-code each time for each software using AI. This just slows things down and introduces errors.
